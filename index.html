Recent Projects

Below you will find some recent projects I have worked on through classwork, data competitions, or my own personal interest.

Predicting Pass Play Results for Professional Football
For my Deep Learning class, I worked with two classmates to build a model to predict EPA and yards gained for NFL plays from the 2018 season. We wanted to see if we could build a model that predicted the result of a pass play from conditions up to the throw. This model would allow us to attribute credit to quarterbacks or wide receivers who consistently achieve higher EPA after the throw when compared to the model's predictions. We compared results for a random forest, XGBoost, and a neural network architecture with the last of the three performing the best. The full report can be found here.

Predicting Balls Hit into Play
As part of my Data Mining class, I worked with two classmates on a semester-long project to find a dataset and build a model. Our group attempted to build a model classifying whether a ball hit into a play resulted in a hit or an out using data from the 2021 MLB season. We compared several types of models in the analysis and also implemented the SMOTE method for dealing with a class imbalance. The full details can be found in our final report here.

Designing a Gameplan for the Golden State Warriors
As part of the executive board of the sports analytics club at Villanova, I helped lead a team in designing this presentation that ultimately won the 2023 Syracuse Basketball Analytics competition. We were tasked with building a gameplan to defend the Golden State Warriors using play-by-play and possession data. We analyzed team and player tendencies, usually using points per possession as a measure of efficiency. We also modeled points over expectation using an XGBoost model to identify areas of the court where the Warriors outperformed the average. I was in charge of the model building along with a portion of the visualizations, and the team gave the presentation to a panel of judges who deemed ours the best ahead of finalists from Tulane, Penn, and Duke.

The Most Confusing Players in Tennis
I was inspired to write this article by a podcast episode trying to identify the players that are the hardest to analyze on the ATP tour. My approach to this question was to try and identify the least consistent players on tour since those would seem to me like the hardest to analyze. My first strategy for doing this was to look at match-to-match variance in player statistics. I also built a couple of models predicting match statistics using lagged player statistics to see which players were consistently the hardest to predict. All results and further discussion can be found in the article here and the code for the article can be found here.

Modeling Expected Points in Python
I have mainly worked in R up until recently, and this is my first project doing data work in Python outside of some spot work in my Deep Learning class. As an R programmer, you will notice most of the code (here) takes advantage of method chaining in pandas at least for the data cleaning. For the project, I built an XGBoost model to predict the points scored on a drive given the circumstances in a play. This methodology underlies the EPA statistic oft-cited in NFL analytics circles. I compare my results to those included in the play-by-play data in the nfl_data_py python package.
